2.1 Definitions

2.1.1 Youth

In this report, we use the term “youth” to refer to individuals of working age (15 to 29). This is consistent with definitions used by Statistics Canada and the International Labour Organization.

2.1.2 Newcomer

This report defines newcomers as immigrants to Canada who landed 10 or fewer years prior to the reference year. Conventional definitions of newcomers use a 5-year window, but the LFS PUMF does not distinguish between immigrants who landed 5 or fewer years ago and those who landed 6 to 10 years ago.

2.1.3 School Months

Current enrollment in either high school or post-secondary education is one dimension of the segmentation model developed below. While the LFS PUMF variable SCHOOLN indicates current attendance, it does indicate current enrollment. This means that, for example, a student who is on summer break would be classified as not currently attending school, even though they are still enrolled. To address this issue, we limit impacted components of the analysis to typically active university terms (September to May).

2.3 How have youth fared in the Canadian labour market?

Unsurprisingly, youth in Canada have lower participation, employment, and earnings than non-youth. Of note is the disproportionate impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on youth employment and participation rates in 2020, and their slower recovery compared to non-youth. Whether this portends a long-term shift in the labour market remains to be seen, but it is clear that youth entering or returning to the labour market in the near future will encounter a different landscape than their pre-pandemic counterparts.

2.3.1 Labour market rate indicators

Table 2.1 presents annualized average rates of participation, employment, unemployment, enrollment, and NEET status among youth in Canada from 2015 to 2024.

It suggests the following:

  • Youth participation and employment rates are consistently lower than those of the non-youth population.
  • NEET rates among youth are consistently lower due to much higher rates of enrollment in education.
  • Youth participation, employment, and NEET rates jumped in 2020, but have largely recovered to pre-pandemic levels.

gt_spanners <- function(g, ...) {
  g_ <- g

  spanners <- rlang::list2(...)

  purrr::iwalk(
    spanners, 
    function(pattern, label) {
      g_ <<- g_ |> 
        gt::tab_spanner(
          label = label, 
          columns = matches(pattern)
        )
    }
  )
  g_
}

lfs_source_note <- function(g, u = "Working-age population (15-64)") {
  g |> 
    gt::tab_source_note(
      source_note = "Source: Labour Force Survey Public Use Microdata File (LFS PUMF), Statistics Canada"
    ) |> 
    gt::tab_source_note(
      source_note = glue::glue("Universe: {u}")
    )
}

2.3.2 Job quality indicators

Table 2.2 shows a selection of job quality indicators among employed youth in Canada from 2015 to 2024.

In it, we observe the following: - Hourly earnings among employed youth are consistently lower than those of their non-youth counterparts. - Employed youth are much more likely to occupy part-time or temporary positions than non-youth employees. - Involuntary part-time work is more common among employed youth than among non-youth employees.

2.4 How has the youth population changed?

The youth population in Canada has grown more educated and more diverse over the past decade. The rate of PSE attainment increased by 9.6%, while the rate of newcomer representation among youth increased by 35.2% over the same period.

2.4.1 National representation of PSE and newcomer status

Table 2.3 shows the proportion of youth in Canada who have attained post-secondary education and the proportion of youth who are newcomers from 2015 to 2024. Both indicators have increased over the past decade. The rate of post-secondary attainment among youth increased by 3.8 ppt between 2015 and 2024, while the rate of newcomer representation among youth has increased by 3.0 ppt over the same period.

2.5 How has youth job-search behaviour changed?

  • Unemployed adults 30+ and youth under 30 share similar job search behaviours
  • Over time, those behaviours have trended in similar directions
  • The only notable difference is that youth have been more likely to reach out to employers directly
